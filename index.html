Reading the ground in light we can't see.

01

The core idea, in one breath

Light bounces off rock differently depending on what minerals are in it. Our eyes read three slices of that light. The satellite reads six — three of them invisible to us — and those invisible slices are exactly where rock chemistry shows up.

A doctor's X-ray sees bone through skin because it uses light beyond human vision. We do the same to the ground: the satellite's infrared sight reads the surface chemistry of rock across all 600 acres in a single pass.

We download cloud-free satellite images of the lease, stack the six bands together, then either run simple chemistry math over them or train a model to highlight the patches whose fingerprint matches our target. The result is a heat-map of spots worth a closer look.

02

Be clear about what this does — and doesn't

This is the slide that keeps us honest. Promising "we'll find the deposit from space" is the fastest way to lose a geologist's trust.

What it can do

  • Map surface rock chemistry and alteration across the whole lease, fast and cheap.
  • Spot clay alteration and iron-staining that often sit near mineralisation.
  • Shrink 600 acres to a handful of priority targets to walk and sample.
  • Re-run over time to compare seasons and acquisitions.

What it can't do

  • See through soil, vegetation, or overburden — it reads the surface skin only.
  • Confirm a deposit; only field sampling and drilling can.
  • See through clouds, or where thick plant cover hides the rock.
  • Replace a geologist's judgement — it gives leads, not conclusions.
The honest goal

Not finding the deposit from orbit — prioritising. Turning a large, undifferentiated lease into a ranked shortlist worth boots and a drill rig.

04

Two ways to find the targets

You don't have to start with deep learning. For one small lease the simple route is often more defensible. Here's the honest trade-off.

Path A · start here

Spectral index maps

Plain band-ratio math. Divide one band by another and it spikes where a target mineral is present. Threshold it, paint it on a map.

Clay Index  = SWIR1 / SWIR2
Ferrous Index = SWIR1 / NIR
  • No training data needed
  • Fully transparent — you can explain every pixel
  • Runs in minutes; ideal for 600 acres
  • Misses subtle multi-band combinations
Path B · scale up later

Fine-tuned AI model

IBM & NASA's Prithvi-EO-2.0 — a foundation model already trained on millions of satellite images. We adapt it to our target rather than train from scratch.

  • Learns patterns indices can't express
  • Improves as you feed it verified results
  • Needs labelled examples of known mineralisation
  • Overfits easily on a tiny area — handle with care

Recommendation: prove the concept with Path A, graduate to Path B once we have ground-truthed labels worth learning from.

06

How the AI model actually works

No magic — a four-part assembly line. The encoder comes pre-built by IBM and NASA; we only build and train the last part.

INPUT

Six-band tiles

The lease image, cut into small squares, each carrying all six light-bands.

ENCODER · PRE-TRAINED

Prithvi backbone

A Vision Transformer that already knows what Earth looks like in these bands. It reads each tile and summarises what's there.

DECODER · WE BUILD

Segmentation head

Paints the encoder's summary back to full size as a per-pixel score: how mineral-likely is this spot?

OUTPUT

Probability map

The lease, every pixel coloured by confidence. The bright spots are our shortlist.

Why Prithvi, not an ordinary image AI? A standard model learns from cat-and-car photos in plain RGB. Prithvi learned from real satellite imagery in these exact six bands — we're hiring an expert and teaching it one new task, not raising a beginner.

07

How to train it properly

The part that separates a real result from a demo that fools us. In order. The red flags are the mistakes that quietly ruin geospatial models.

Define the target and gather labels

Decide exactly what we're hunting, then collect known examples — past drill hits, mapped occurrences, field-verified spots. A model can only learn from labelled truth.

No labels? Don't fake it — fall back to Path A index maps or unsupervised anomaly detection.

Acquire and clean the imagery

Pull cloud-free Sentinel-2 / Landsat / HLS scenes with all six bands. Apply atmospheric correction so values mean the same across dates, and align every band to one resolution.

Cut into tiles and normalise

Chop the scene into model-sized tiles and scale band values into the range the model expects. Consistency here prevents a lot of mysterious failures later.

Split the data spatially — not randomly

Hold out a whole separate region for testing. Neighbouring pixels are nearly identical, so a random split lets the model peek at the answers and report fictional scores.

Random train/test split = the #1 way to get a great-looking model that's useless in the field.

Fine-tune the model

Keep Prithvi's knowledge mostly frozen and train the new decoder head with a small learning rate, augmentation and early stopping. On 600 acres the danger is memorising, not learning — keep it on a short leash.

Use a loss built for rarity

Targets are a tiny fraction of all pixels. Combine Binary Cross-Entropy with Dice loss so the model is rewarded for finding rare positives, not for labelling everything "empty" and scoring 99%.

Validate honestly on held-out ground

Judge it only on the region it never trained on, using IoU, F1 and the precision/recall trade-off from the simulator. A model that aces training and flops on new land learned nothing.

Predict across the full lease

Run the trained model over every tile to produce the probability heat-map and rank the targets.

Ground-truth, then feed it back

Send crews to the top targets. Every confirmation or miss becomes a new label that improves the next model. The map is a hypothesis until the field proves it.

09

Plain-English glossary

Band
One slice of light at a specific wavelength the satellite records separately.
Multispectral
Capturing several bands at once, including invisible infrared.
NIR
Near-infrared. Just past red; plants are very bright here.
SWIR
Short-wave infrared. Deeper infrared where clays and iron minerals reveal themselves.
Spectral index
A band-ratio that spikes where a target mineral is present.
Foundation model
A large model pre-trained on huge data that you adapt instead of training from zero.
Prithvi-EO-2.0
IBM & NASA's Earth-observation foundation model, trained on satellite imagery.
Vision Transformer
The model's design: reads an image as a grid of patches and learns how they relate.
Fine-tuning
Lightly retraining a pre-built model for a specific new job.
Segmentation
Labelling every pixel — here, scoring each as mineral-likely or not.
Overfitting
Memorising the training data instead of the pattern; useless on new ground.
Spatial split
Testing on a geographically separate area so scores aren't inflated by lookalike neighbours.
IoU / Dice
Scores for how well predicted areas overlap the true areas.
Ground-truth
Real-world checking — field sampling or drilling that confirms the map.
